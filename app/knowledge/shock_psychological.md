# Shock, Panic & Psychological First Aid

## MEDICAL SHOCK (Life-Threatening)

### What Is Shock?
Shock is when the body's organs don't get enough blood flow. It kills quickly without treatment.
NOT the same as emotional shock — medical shock is a physical emergency.

### Types of Shock
- **Hypovolemic** (most common in war): Blood loss from wounds, dehydration, severe burns
- **Cardiogenic**: Heart failure, heart attack
- **Septic**: Severe infection spreading through bloodstream
- **Anaphylactic**: Severe allergic reaction
- **Neurogenic**: Spinal cord injury disrupts circulation

### Signs of Shock
EARLY signs:
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Rapid breathing
- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Anxiety, restlessness, confusion
- Nausea, vomiting
- Thirst

LATE signs (CRITICAL — act immediately):
- Very weak or absent pulse
- Blue-gray skin (especially lips and fingertips)
- Staring eyes, dilated pupils
- Unconsciousness
- Breathing slowing or stopping

### Shock Treatment (No Equipment)
1. Call for medical help immediately if available
2. Lay the person DOWN on their back
3. Elevate legs 15-30 cm (6-12 inches) — this pushes blood to vital organs
   - Do NOT elevate legs if: head injury, spinal injury, broken leg, difficulty breathing
4. Stop any visible bleeding (direct pressure)
5. Keep the person WARM — cover with blankets, coats, anything available
6. Do NOT give food or drink (they may need surgery)
   - Exception: If help is hours away and person is conscious, small sips of water
7. Loosen tight clothing (belts, collars, shoe laces)
8. Stay with the person — talk to them calmly, keep them awake
9. If they vomit or become unconscious, turn to recovery position (on their side)
10. Monitor breathing and pulse continuously

### Anaphylaxis (Severe Allergic Reaction)
Signs: Swelling of face/throat, difficulty breathing, hives, rapid pulse, dizziness
TREATMENT:
1. Use epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if available — inject into outer thigh through clothing
2. Lay person down, elevate legs
3. If breathing difficulty: let them sit up slightly
4. Call emergency services
5. If no auto-injector: antihistamine (Benadryl) helps MILD reactions only
6. Be ready for CPR if breathing stops
7. A second reaction can occur 4-12 hours later — keep monitoring

## PSYCHOLOGICAL FIRST AID

### Why Psychological First Aid Matters
In war zones, mental trauma can be as disabling as physical injury. Panic causes poor decisions that get people killed. Psychological first aid saves lives by keeping people functional.

### Panic Attacks
Signs: Racing heart, chest pain, difficulty breathing, feeling of dying, shaking, tingling hands/feet, overwhelming fear

TREATMENT:
1. Stay calm yourself — panic is contagious, but so is calm
2. Move the person to a quieter area if safe
3. Speak in a slow, firm, reassuring voice
4. Have them breathe: "Breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, breathe out for 6"
5. Ground them: "Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear"
6. Physical grounding: Have them press feet firmly into ground, hold something cold
7. Reassure: "This is a panic attack. It feels terrible but it WILL pass. You are not dying."
8. Stay with them until it passes (usually 5-20 minutes)
9. Do NOT slap, shake, or throw water — these increase panic

### Acute Stress Reactions (During/After Traumatic Events)
Normal reactions to abnormal events:
- Crying, screaming, trembling
- Frozen/unable to move (freeze response)
- Hypervigilance (jumping at every sound)
- Numbness/feeling detached
- Difficulty speaking or thinking clearly
- Nausea, dizziness, rapid heartbeat

TREATMENT:
1. Ensure physical safety first — move away from danger
2. Meet basic needs: water, food, warmth, rest
3. Calm presence — sit near them, don't force conversation
4. Simple, clear instructions: "Drink this water." "Sit here." "You are safe now."
5. Let them talk if they want to — listen without judging or advising
6. Do NOT say: "I know how you feel" / "It could be worse" / "Be strong"
7. DO say: "You survived." / "You are safe now." / "It's normal to feel this way."
8. Respect silence — some people need quiet

### Frozen/Paralyzed by Fear
When someone is unable to move or respond:
1. Get down to their eye level
2. Say their name firmly: "Ahmed. Ahmed. Look at me."
3. Make gentle physical contact — touch their shoulder or hand
4. Give ONE simple instruction: "Stand up" or "Take my hand"
5. Once they respond to one instruction, give the next
6. Keep voice calm but authoritative
7. Lead them physically if needed — guide with your hand

### Children in Trauma
Children respond differently to crisis:
- May regress (bed-wetting, thumb-sucking, baby talk)
- Clinginess, refusing to separate from caregiver
- Nightmares, sleep problems
- Aggression or withdrawal
- Repetitive play about the traumatic event (this is NORMAL processing)

TREATMENT:
1. Maintain routines as much as possible (mealtimes, bedtimes)
2. Physical comfort — hold them, hug, maintain physical contact
3. Simple honest explanations appropriate to age
4. Reassure: "It's my job to keep you safe. I am here."
5. Let them play — play is how children process trauma
6. Limit exposure to disturbing conversations and scenes
7. Keep family together — separation increases trauma dramatically

### Grief and Loss
When someone has lost family, friends, home:
1. Be present — just being there matters more than words
2. Allow expression of emotion — crying, anger, silence are all normal
3. Help with practical tasks (food, water, shelter)
4. Do NOT rush them through grief
5. Watch for signs of suicidal thoughts: giving away possessions, talking about dying, sudden calm after despair
6. If suicidal: stay with them, remove means of harm, do not leave them alone

### Preventing Mental Health Breakdown
- Stay connected to other people — isolation worsens trauma
- Maintain daily structure (wake time, meals, tasks)
- Physical activity helps process stress hormones
- Focus on what you CAN control
- Limit constant exposure to traumatic scenes
- Help others — giving aid improves your own mental state
- Accept that fear, anger, and sadness are NORMAL responses
- Sleep is critical — take turns keeping watch so everyone can rest

## HEAT AND COLD EMERGENCIES

### Heat Exhaustion
Signs: Heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, fast weak pulse, nausea, fainting
TREATMENT:
1. Move to coolest available area (shade, indoors)
2. Lie down, elevate legs
3. Remove excess clothing
4. Cool with wet cloths on neck, armpits, groin
5. Sip water slowly
6. Fan the person

### Heat Stroke (LIFE-THREATENING)
Signs: Body temperature over 40°C/104°F, hot red dry skin (NO sweating), rapid strong pulse, confusion, unconsciousness
TREATMENT:
1. This is an EMERGENCY — cool the person immediately
2. Move to shade or indoors
3. Immerse in cold water if possible
4. Pack ice/cold cloths on neck, armpits, and groin
5. Fan while spraying with water
6. Do NOT give fluids if unconscious
7. Can cause seizures — protect head, clear area

### Hypothermia (Body Too Cold)
Signs: Shivering (stops in severe cases), confusion, slow speech, drowsiness, weak pulse
TREATMENT:
1. Move to warmest available shelter
2. Remove wet clothing
3. Warm the CENTER of body first: chest, neck, head, groin
4. Use body heat: skin-to-skin contact under blankets
5. Warm (not hot) drinks if conscious
6. Do NOT rub extremities — can cause heart failure
7. Do NOT use direct heat (fire, hot water) — warm gradually
8. Handle gently — rough movement can trigger cardiac arrest in severe hypothermia
