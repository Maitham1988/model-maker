# Shelter, Warmth, Navigation & Rescue Signals

## EMERGENCY SHELTER

### Priorities in Order
1. Protection from wind (wind chill kills fastest)
2. Protection from rain/moisture
3. Insulation from cold ground
4. Shade from sun (in hot climates)
A bad shelter is better than no shelter.

### Quick Shelters (Under 30 Minutes)

#### Debris Hut
1. Find a long sturdy pole (ridgepole) — length of your body + 30cm
2. Prop one end on a rock, stump, or Y-shaped branch at hip height
3. Lean shorter branches along both sides at 45° angle
4. Layer leaves, grass, cardboard, plastic — anything — over the branches
5. Make the layer THICK (30cm+ for cold weather)
6. Fill inside with dry leaves/grass for insulation bedding
7. Make it just big enough for your body — smaller = warmer

#### Tarp/Plastic Sheet Shelter
1. Tie rope/string between two supports (trees, poles, walls)
2. Drape tarp/plastic over the rope
3. Stake or weigh down edges with rocks
4. Angle at 45° to shed water
5. If raining: dig a small trench around the shelter to divert water

#### Vehicle Shelter
1. Car/truck provides excellent wind and rain protection
2. Run engine briefly for heat (10-15 minutes per hour MAX)
3. DANGER: Keep a window slightly open to prevent carbon monoxide buildup
4. Clear exhaust pipe of snow/debris before running engine
5. Use floor mats, seat covers, and trunk contents for insulation

#### Urban Emergency Shelters
- Stairwells (protected from wind, structurally strong)
- Basements (protection from everything except flooding)
- Under concrete overhang or bridges (temporary only)
- Abandoned vehicles
- Large dumpsters (if clean and empty — good wind protection)
- Between buildings in narrow alleys (wind protection)
- Pile furniture/mattresses for insulation walls

### Ground Insulation Is Critical
The ground steals body heat 30x faster than air. ALWAYS insulate:
- Cardboard (several layers)
- Dry leaves, grass, straw (30cm+ thick)
- Branches/pine needles
- Newspapers (crumple, don't lay flat)
- Foam, carpet, rugs, mattresses
- Plastic bags stuffed with dry material

### Waterproofing
- Plastic bags, trash bags, tarps over any shelter
- Layer large leaves like roof tiles (overlapping, starting from bottom)
- Mud and grass mixture plastered over framework (daub)
- Vehicle hoods, doors, or panels as roofing

## FIRE AND WARMTH

### Starting a Fire

#### With Matches or Lighter
1. Prepare BEFORE striking: gather tinder (dry grass, paper, lint, cotton, bark shavings)
2. Build small nest of tinder
3. Light tinder
4. Add thin twigs (pencil-thin), then thicker sticks gradually
5. Do NOT smother — fire needs air flow

#### Without Matches (Difficult but Possible)
- Battery + steel wool: Touch both battery terminals to fine steel wool — it will ignite instantly
- Battery + foil: Fold foil into thin strip connecting both battery terminals — it will glow and ignite tinder
- Car battery: Touch jumper cables together near tinder — sparks ignite
- Magnifying glass/eyeglasses/water-filled bottle: Focus sunlight to a point on dark tinder
- Flint and steel: Strike steel against flint at 45° with fast downward stroke — catch sparks in char cloth or fine tinder

### Fire Safety
- Build fire on bare ground, rock, or concrete (not on roots or peat)
- Ring of rocks around fire prevents spread
- Keep fire manageable — small fires are easier to control and conceal
- Have water or dirt ready to extinguish
- Never leave fire unattended
- In conflict zones: fire and smoke signals your position — consider concealment

### Warmth Without Fire
- Insulation: Stuff newspaper, dry leaves, or plastic bags inside clothing
- Layer clothing: Multiple thin layers trap more heat than one thick layer
- Cover head: 30-40% of heat loss is through the head
- Keep dry: Wet clothing loses 90% of insulating value
- Huddle together: Share body heat with others
- Exercise: But avoid sweating — sweat + cold = dangerous
- Hot water bottle: Heat water, pour into sealed bottle/container, hold against body
- Chemical hand warmers: Shake to activate if available

## NAVIGATION WITHOUT TOOLS

### Direction Finding

#### Using the Sun (Northern Hemisphere — Bahrain, Middle East, Europe, North America)
- Sun rises approximately in EAST
- Sun sets approximately in WEST
- At midday, sun is due SOUTH
- Shadows point NORTH at midday

#### Stick Shadow Method (Accurate)
1. Place a straight stick vertically in the ground
2. Mark the tip of its shadow with a stone
3. Wait 15-30 minutes
4. Mark the NEW shadow tip
5. Draw a line between the two marks: this is an EAST-WEST line
6. First mark is WEST, second mark is EAST
7. Stand with WEST to your left — you are facing NORTH

#### Using Stars (Northern Hemisphere)
- Find the Big Dipper (Ursa Major) — 7 bright stars in a "pot" shape
- The two stars at the end of the "pot" point to the North Star (Polaris)
- Polaris is at the tip of the Little Dipper's handle
- Polaris is always TRUE NORTH

#### Natural Navigation Clues
- Moss tends to grow on the NORTH side of trees and rocks (more shade/moisture)
- Spider webs tend to face SOUTH (warmer)
- Snow melts first on SOUTH-facing slopes
- Satellite dishes point SOUTH (in Northern Hemisphere) toward equatorial satellites
- Mosque qibla direction points toward Makkah (south-southeast from Bahrain/Gulf)

### Staying Found
- STAY where you are if possible — moving makes rescue harder
- If you must move: leave markers (piled rocks, arrows on ground, tied cloth on branches)
- Follow water downstream — it leads to civilization
- Follow power lines — they lead to people
- Walk along roads, never away from them

## RESCUE SIGNALS

### Universal Distress Signals
- **3 of anything**: 3 fires in a triangle, 3 whistle blasts, 3 gunshots, 3 light flashes
- **SOS**: Three short signals, three long signals, three short signals (· · · – – – · · ·)
- **Ground signals**: Write "SOS" or "HELP" large enough to see from aircraft (minimum 3 meters tall)

### Visual Signals
- Mirror/reflective surface: Aim reflected sunlight at aircraft or distant people
- Bright colored cloth/clothing: Spread on open ground or hang from elevated point
- Fire and smoke: Daytime — add green branches/leaves for thick white smoke. Nighttime — bright flames
- Signal fire: Prepare 3 fires in a triangle, light all when rescue is spotted
- Flashlight: Flash SOS at night toward aircraft or ships

### Ground-to-Air Signals (International)
These symbols should be at least 3 meters tall, made from contrasting material:
- V = Need Assistance
- X = Need Medical Help
- I = Need Supplies
- → = Traveling This Direction
- N = No (if asked questions by aircraft)
- Y = Yes

### Audible Signals
- Whistle: 3 blasts repeated — travels farther than shouting
- Banging metal on metal: Carries far in open areas
- Car horn: 3 short bursts repeated
- Shouting: Cup hands around mouth, face direction of potential rescuers

### Electronic Signals (If Any Power Available)
- Mobile phone: Even without signal, call emergency numbers (often work on any network). Send text messages (uses less power than calls). Turn on location services.
- Radio: Transmit "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY" on any frequency, give your location
- Vehicle headlights: Flash SOS pattern
- Any electronic screen: Flash on and off to attract attention at night

## ROPE AND KNOTS

### Essential Knots

#### Bowline (Rescue Loop — Won't Tighten)
- Creates a fixed loop that doesn't slip or tighten under load
- Use for: rescue harness, tying to a post, securing objects
- Memory aid: "The rabbit comes out of the hole, around the tree, and back down the hole"

#### Clove Hitch (Quick Tie to a Post)
- Wraps around posts, poles, or branches
- Quick to tie and release
- Good for starting lashing (tying poles together)

#### Square Knot (Joining Two Ropes)
- Ties two ropes of EQUAL thickness together
- Left over right, then right over left
- Used for bandage knots, tying bundles

### Improvised Rope/Cordage
- Twisted strips of cloth or clothing
- Electrical wire (stripped from buildings/vehicles)
- Plant fibers: twisted grass, bark strips, vines
- Seat belts (extremely strong)
- Paracord from backpacks, hoods, or jacket drawstrings
- Shoelaces
- Dental floss (surprisingly strong for thin cord)
