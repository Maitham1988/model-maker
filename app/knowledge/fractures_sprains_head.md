# Fractures, Sprains & Head Injuries

## BROKEN BONES (FRACTURES)

### How to Tell If a Bone Is Broken
- Severe pain that worsens with movement
- Swelling, bruising, tenderness
- Visible deformity (limb looks bent or shortened)
- Inability to bear weight or use the limb
- Grinding or crackling sensation (crepitus)
- Bone poking through skin (open/compound fracture — VERY serious)
NOTE: You cannot always tell the difference between a fracture and a bad sprain without X-ray. Treat suspected fractures AS fractures.

### General Fracture Treatment (Without Medical Supplies)
1. Do NOT try to straighten or push bones back
2. Immobilize the limb IN THE POSITION YOU FOUND IT
3. Splint above AND below the fracture site
4. Check circulation below the injury: can they feel fingers/toes? Are they warm? Is there a pulse?

### Improvised Splints
Use any rigid material:
- Sticks, branches, broom handles, straight pieces of wood
- Rolled-up magazine or newspaper (surprisingly rigid)
- Cardboard folded thick
- Metal pipes, umbrella, ruler
- Even a pillow or blanket rolled tight and wrapped around limb
- For fingers: tape injured finger to neighboring finger (buddy taping)
- For arm: fold a sling from cloth, shirt, or scarf

PADDING: Always pad between the splint and skin with cloth, clothing, or soft material to prevent pressure sores.

### Specific Fracture First Aid

#### Arm/Wrist Fracture
1. Place arm across chest in comfortable position
2. Splint from elbow to beyond the wrist
3. Make a sling: triangular cloth or fold a shirt, tie around neck
4. Secure sling with a band around the body

#### Leg Fracture (Shin/Thigh)
1. Do NOT allow the person to walk
2. Place padding between the legs
3. Splint the injured leg by strapping it to the uninjured leg (buddy splinting)
4. Or use two rigid splints — one on each side of the leg, extending from hip to ankle
5. Tie at 3-4 points above and below the fracture (never directly on the break)

#### Collarbone Fracture
1. Support the arm on the injured side with a sling
2. Do NOT try to push the bone back
3. Person will naturally hold arm close to body — let them

#### Rib Fracture
1. Do NOT bind or wrap the chest tightly — this restricts breathing
2. Have person hold a pillow or folded cloth against the injured area for comfort
3. Encourage gentle breathing — avoid deep breaths that cause pain
4. Watch for difficulty breathing, which could indicate a punctured lung
5. Lean or lie on the INJURED side — this restricts movement of broken ribs and reduces pain

#### Pelvic Fracture (Very Serious)
1. DO NOT move the person unless in immediate danger
2. Keep them lying flat, knees slightly bent with support underneath
3. Do NOT roll them or let them try to stand
4. Can cause severe internal bleeding — life-threatening

## OPEN (COMPOUND) FRACTURE — BONE THROUGH SKIN
This is a high-risk emergency. Infection risk is extreme.
1. Do NOT push the bone back in
2. Cover the wound with the cleanest material available
3. Moisten the covering to prevent bone from drying out
4. Build a splint around the protruding bone — create a frame that doesn't touch it
5. Control bleeding with pressure AROUND the wound, not on the bone
6. This person MUST get to a hospital — high risk of life-threatening infection

## DISLOCATED JOINTS

### Signs of Dislocation
- Joint looks visibly out of place or deformed
- Severe pain
- Inability to move the joint
- Swelling and bruising

### Treatment
1. Do NOT try to pop it back in — you can damage nerves and blood vessels
2. Immobilize the joint in the position you find it
3. Apply cold if available (wrapped cloth with cold water, ice, cold earth)
4. Support with a sling or splint
5. Exception: Shoulder dislocations — trained people can reduce these, but if untrained, immobilize and seek help

## SPRAINS AND STRAINS

### RICE Method (when no medical supplies available)
- **R**est — Stop using the injured area
- **I**ce — Apply cold: wet cloth, cold water, cold mud, cold canned food
- **C**ompression — Wrap firmly (not too tight) with cloth, bandage, or torn clothing
- **E**levation — Raise the injured limb above heart level

### Ankle Sprain (Most Common)
1. Do NOT remove the shoe immediately if there's no open wound — the shoe acts as compression
2. If shoe is removed, wrap the ankle in a figure-8 pattern with cloth
3. Can the person walk? If yes, it's likely a sprain. If no, it may be a fracture — treat as fracture

## HEAD INJURIES

### Mild Head Injury (Concussion)
Signs: Headache, dizziness, brief confusion, nausea, ringing in ears, sensitivity to light
1. Monitor closely for 24 hours
2. Keep person awake for the first 2-4 hours
3. Ask simple questions periodically (name, date, location) to check mental function
4. Rest in a quiet, dark room
5. Do NOT give aspirin or blood thinners — increases bleeding risk
6. Apply cold pack to bump/bruise

### Severe Head Injury — EMERGENCY
Signs: Loss of consciousness, seizure, clear fluid from ears/nose, unequal pupils, repeated vomiting, worsening confusion, slurred speech, weakness on one side of body
1. Assume spinal injury — do NOT move the head or neck
2. Keep person lying flat, head slightly elevated if no spinal injury suspected
3. If unconscious but breathing: recovery position (but support head/neck alignment)
4. If not breathing: CPR (but keep neck as still as possible)
5. Control bleeding from scalp wounds — scalp bleeds heavily but is rarely life-threatening
6. Clear fluid from ears/nose = possible skull fracture — do NOT plug the ears or nose

### Scalp Wounds
The scalp has many blood vessels and bleeds profusely even from small cuts.
1. Apply firm pressure with clean cloth
2. If you can see bone or the wound is gaping, hold edges together
3. Hair tie technique: gather hair from each side of wound, tie across to close it
4. Most scalp bleeding stops with firm pressure in 10-15 minutes

## SPINAL INJURY

### When to Suspect Spinal Injury
- Fall from height
- Explosion or blast
- Vehicle accident
- Injury to head/face/neck
- Person complains of neck/back pain
- Tingling, numbness, or inability to move arms/legs

### Management
1. DO NOT MOVE THE PERSON unless they are in immediate life-threatening danger (fire, collapse)
2. Tell them to stay completely still
3. If you MUST move them: log-roll technique — keep head, neck, and spine aligned as ONE unit
4. Support the head with your hands on either side, keeping it in neutral position
5. If available, place rolled towels/clothing on each side of the head to prevent movement
