# Bites, Stings, Poisoning & Allergic Reactions

## SNAKE BITES

### General Snake Bite First Aid
Most snake bites are NOT venomous. But treat EVERY bite as potentially venomous until proven otherwise.

1. Move away from the snake — do NOT try to catch or kill it
2. Keep the person CALM and STILL — movement spreads venom faster
3. Lay them down with the bite BELOW heart level
4. Remove rings, watches, tight clothing near the bite — swelling will occur
5. Clean the bite gently with clean water (do NOT scrub)
6. Immobilize the bitten limb like a fracture — splint or sling
7. Mark the edge of swelling with a pen/marker and note the time
8. Monitor: breathing, pulse, swelling progression
9. Get to medical facility IMMEDIATELY

### What NOT to Do (Snake Bites)
- Do NOT cut the wound
- Do NOT suck out venom (does NOT work, risks infection)
- Do NOT apply a tourniquet (causes tissue death)
- Do NOT apply ice (increases tissue damage)
- Do NOT give alcohol (speeds venom spread)
- Do NOT raise the bite above the heart
- Do NOT apply electric shock (myth, does nothing)

### Pressure Immobilization (For Neurotoxic Snakes — cobras, mambas, sea snakes)
1. Apply a firm bandage over the bite site (NOT tourniquet tight)
2. Wrap the ENTIRE limb starting from fingers/toes upward
3. Tension: same as wrapping a sprained ankle — firm but can slide a finger under
4. Splint the limb to prevent ALL movement
5. This slows venom spread through lymph system
6. Do NOT remove bandage until at medical facility with antivenom available

### Signs of Venomous Bite
- Local: Rapid swelling, intense pain, bruising, blistering, bleeding from wound that won't stop
- Systemic: Nausea, vomiting, blurred vision, difficulty breathing, metallic taste, heavy sweating
- Neurotoxic: Drooping eyelids, difficulty swallowing, slurred speech, weakness, paralysis
- Blood toxin: Bleeding from gums, nose, or existing wounds — blood won't clot

## SCORPION STINGS

### Treatment
1. Wash the sting area with soap and water
2. Apply cold compress for 10 minutes on, 10 minutes off
3. Keep the affected limb elevated if possible
4. Pain management: Ibuprofen or paracetamol
5. Monitor for severe reaction (especially in children)

### Severe Scorpion Sting Signs (Seek Immediate Help)
- Muscle twitching or spasms
- Unusual head, neck, or eye movements
- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Drooling
- Restlessness, sweating
- Nausea, vomiting
- Fast heart rate, high blood pressure
- CHILDREN are at highest risk — a sting that is mild in an adult can be deadly in a child

## SPIDER BITES

### General Treatment
1. Clean the bite with soap and water
2. Apply cold compress
3. Elevate the affected area
4. Take pain reliever
5. Watch for worsening symptoms over 24-48 hours

### Signs of Dangerous Spider Bite
- Expanding area of redness or darkness around the bite
- Severe pain that increases over hours
- Fever, chills, body aches
- Open sore or blister forming at bite site
- Stomach cramps, nausea, muscle pain (possible systemic envenomation)

## INSECT BITES AND STINGS (Bees, Wasps, Hornets, Ants)

### Standard Treatment
1. Remove stinger if visible — scrape sideways with a flat edge (credit card, knife blade)
   - Do NOT squeeze with tweezers (injects more venom)
2. Wash with soap and water
3. Apply cold compress for 10 minutes
4. Elevate if swelling
5. Take antihistamine for itching (if available)
6. Apply baking soda paste or vinegar for pain relief

### Multiple Stings
More than 10 stings in an adult (fewer in children) can cause toxic reaction even without allergy:
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- Headache, dizziness
- Fever
- Muscle pain
- Seizures (rare but possible)
TREATMENT: Seek medical help, monitor breathing, prepare for anaphylaxis.

### Anaphylaxis from Insect Stings
Signs: Swelling of face/throat, difficulty breathing, hives all over body, dizziness, rapid weak pulse
TREATMENT:
1. EpiPen if available — inject into outer thigh
2. Lay person down, elevate legs
3. If breathing difficulty, let them sit up slightly
4. Give antihistamine if they can swallow
5. Be prepared for CPR
6. Reaction can return in 4-12 hours — keep monitoring

## DOG AND ANIMAL BITES

### Treatment
1. Control bleeding with direct pressure
2. Clean the wound thoroughly with soap and running water for at least 5 minutes
3. Do NOT close the wound with tape or stitches (traps bacteria)
4. Cover with clean bandage
5. Watch for infection: redness, swelling, warmth, pus, fever
6. Change dressing daily

### Rabies Risk
- ANY warm-blooded animal can carry rabies
- Especially: dogs, cats, bats, foxes, monkeys, raccoons
- Rabies is ALWAYS fatal once symptoms appear
- Symptoms start 1-3 months after bite (sometimes longer)
- Only prevention: post-exposure vaccination within days of bite
- Wash the wound with soap immediately — this alone reduces rabies risk by 90%

## HUMAN BITES
- More infection-prone than animal bites (human mouth has many bacteria)
- Clean thoroughly with soap and water
- Do NOT close the wound
- Very high risk of infection — watch closely
- Antibiotics usually needed

## POISONING

### Swallowed Poison
Signs: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, burning in mouth/throat, difficulty breathing, confusion, unconsciousness

TREATMENT:
1. Identify the poison if possible — save containers, labels, or samples
2. If conscious and alert: rinse mouth with water, spit it out
3. Do NOT induce vomiting unless specifically instructed — vomiting can cause more damage with corrosive substances
4. If they swallowed CORROSIVE substance (bleach, drain cleaner, acid): Do NOT induce vomiting — give small sips of water or milk to dilute
5. If they swallowed NON-CORROSIVE substance (medications, certain plants): Contact poison control if available
6. If unconscious: place in recovery position, check breathing
7. If not breathing: CPR
8. Save any vomit for medical identification

### Inhaled Poison (Gas, Fumes, Smoke)
1. Move person to fresh air IMMEDIATELY
2. Protect yourself — do NOT enter gas-filled area without protection
3. Open doors and windows if possible
4. If not breathing: CPR (use barrier if available to avoid breathing in poison yourself)
5. Loosen tight clothing
6. Keep warm and calm

### Skin Contact with Poison (Chemicals)
1. Remove contaminated clothing (wearing gloves if possible)
2. Flush skin with large amounts of running water for 15-20 minutes
3. Wash with soap gently — do NOT scrub (forces chemical deeper)
4. Do NOT apply creams or ointments unless instructed
5. Watch for chemical burn signs: reddening, blistering, pain

### Eye Contact with Chemicals
1. Flush eye with clean lukewarm water for AT LEAST 15 minutes
2. Hold eyelid open — the person will instinctively try to close it
3. Tilt head so water runs AWAY from the other eye
4. Remove contact lenses if present (after starting flush)
5. Do NOT rub the eye
6. Cover with clean damp cloth after flushing
7. Seek medical help

## COMMON PLANT POISONING
- Symptoms range from mouth/throat irritation to organ failure
- If a child eats an unknown plant: check mouth for remaining pieces, rinse mouth, save a sample of the plant
- Symptoms may take hours to appear
- Treatment is mostly supportive: hydration, monitoring, seek medical help

## FOOD AND MUSHROOM POISONING
- Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps
- Onset: 30 minutes to 12 hours after eating (mushrooms can take 6-24 hours)
- DANGEROUS mushroom poisoning signs: symptoms starting 6+ hours after eating, followed by a period of FEELING BETTER, then liver/kidney failure
- Treatment: Oral rehydration (water + salt + sugar), rest, seek medical care for severe cases
- Save a sample of the suspected food/mushroom for identification

## MEDICATION OVERDOSE
- Signs vary by drug: drowsiness, confusion, slow/fast breathing, pinpoint/dilated pupils, seizures
- DO: Check breathing, place in recovery position, call for help
- DO: Note what was taken, how much, and when
- DO NOT: Induce vomiting unless instructed
- Opioid overdose (very slow breathing, pinpoint pupils): naloxone (Narcan) if available — spray in nose or inject
