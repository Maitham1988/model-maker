# Breathing, Choking & CPR

## CHOKING — ADULT OR CHILD (CONSCIOUS)
Someone gasping, clutching throat, turning blue, unable to speak or cough.

### Heimlich Maneuver (Abdominal Thrusts)
1. Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist
2. Make a fist with one hand, place thumb side above their belly button, below ribcage
3. Grab your fist with your other hand
4. Pull sharply inward and upward — like a J-shaped motion
5. Repeat rapidly until the object comes out or they lose consciousness
6. Each thrust should be a separate, forceful motion
NOTE: For pregnant women or very obese people — do chest thrusts instead (arms around chest, fist on breastbone).

### Self-Choking (Alone)
1. Make a fist, place it above your belly button
2. Lean over a hard object — chair back, countertop, or railing
3. Drive your fist inward and upward using the object as leverage
4. Repeat forcefully until the object dislodges

### Infant Choking (Under 1 Year)
1. Place baby face-down on your forearm, head lower than body
2. Support head by holding the jaw
3. Give 5 firm back blows between shoulder blades with the heel of your hand
4. If not cleared: turn baby face-up, give 5 chest thrusts using 2 fingers on center of chest
5. Alternate between 5 back blows and 5 chest thrusts
6. Do NOT do abdominal thrusts on infants — risk of organ damage
7. If baby becomes unconscious, start infant CPR

## CPR — ADULT

### When to Do CPR
- Person is unresponsive (not reacting to voice or taps)
- Not breathing (or only gasping)
- No pulse (check neck/wrist for 10 seconds max)
START CPR IMMEDIATELY. Every minute without CPR reduces survival by 10%.

### CPR Steps (Hands-Only)
1. Place person flat on their back on a firm surface
2. Kneel beside their chest
3. Place the heel of one hand on the CENTER of the chest (between the nipples)
4. Place your other hand on top, fingers interlocked
5. Lock your elbows, position shoulders directly above your hands
6. Push HARD and FAST — compress at least 2 inches (5 cm) deep
7. Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute (rhythm of "Stayin' Alive" song)
8. Allow chest to fully recoil between compressions
9. Do NOT stop — continue until the person revives, help arrives, or you are physically unable

### CPR with Rescue Breaths (if trained)
1. After every 30 compressions, tilt head back and lift chin
2. Pinch nose shut, seal your mouth over theirs
3. Give 2 breaths, each lasting 1 second — watch for chest rise
4. Immediately resume compressions
5. Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 breaths
If you are not comfortable giving breaths, hands-only CPR is still effective.

### Infant CPR (Under 1 Year)
1. Use 2 fingers (index + middle) instead of palm
2. Compress about 1.5 inches (4 cm) deep
3. Give 30 compressions then 2 gentle breaths (cover mouth AND nose)
4. Breaths should be gentle puffs — infant lungs are small

## DIFFICULTY BREATHING

### Causes in War Zones
- Smoke or dust inhalation
- Chemical/gas exposure
- Chest injury (broken ribs, punctured lung)
- Panic attack / anxiety
- Asthma attack
- Allergic reaction (anaphylaxis)

### Smoke/Dust Inhalation
1. Move to fresh air immediately — get LOW (smoke rises)
2. Cover mouth and nose with damp cloth if possible
3. Breathe through the cloth — filters larger particles
4. Sit in an upright position — helps lungs expand
5. If available, rinse mouth and nose with clean water
6. Cough gently to clear airways — do NOT suppress coughing
7. Watch for worsening symptoms: wheezing, coughing blood, voice changes

### Chest Injury with Breathing Difficulty
If air is escaping from a chest wound (sucking chest wound — you hear air going in/out of wound):
1. SEAL the wound immediately — this is life-threatening
2. Use plastic (bag, wrap, packaging) to cover the wound
3. Tape on THREE sides only — leave the bottom edge open
4. This creates a one-way valve: air escapes but cannot enter
5. Position person with injured side DOWN (if conscious, let them choose comfortable position)
6. Monitor breathing constantly

### Panic Attack vs. Real Emergency
Panic attack symptoms: rapid breathing, tingling hands/lips, feeling of doom, racing heart, but ABLE to speak in full sentences.
Real breathing emergency: blue lips, unable to speak more than 1-2 words, flaring nostrils, sucking in between ribs, altered consciousness.

### Helping Someone Hyperventilating (Panic)
1. Stay calm yourself — your calm is contagious
2. Speak in a slow, steady voice
3. Guide them: "Breathe with me — in through nose for 4 seconds, out through mouth for 6 seconds"
4. Have them focus on ONE thing they can see, touch, or smell
5. Do NOT use a paper bag — this is outdated and can be dangerous
6. Reassure them: "You are safe. This will pass."

## ASTHMA ATTACK (NO INHALER AVAILABLE)

### Emergency Steps
1. Sit upright — NEVER lie down during an asthma attack
2. Stay calm — panic worsens bronchospasm
3. Lean slightly forward, hands on knees (tripod position)
4. Breathe slowly through nose, out through pursed lips
5. Coffee or strong tea (caffeine) can help open airways mildly — drink if available
6. Move away from triggers: dust, smoke, cold air
7. Loosen any tight clothing around chest and neck
8. If symptoms worsen (cannot speak, blue lips): this is a medical emergency

## DROWNING / WATER IN LUNGS

### After Pulling Someone from Water
1. Check for breathing and pulse immediately
2. If not breathing: start CPR right away
3. Do NOT try to "pump water out" by pressing on stomach
4. Position on side (recovery position) if breathing — water will drain naturally
5. Even if person seems fine after near-drowning, monitor for 24 hours
6. "Secondary drowning" can occur hours later — water in lungs causes delayed swelling
WARNING SIGNS after near-drowning: persistent coughing, difficulty breathing, extreme fatigue, confusion — these are medical emergencies.
