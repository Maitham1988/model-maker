# Bleeding Control & Wound Care

## SEVERE BLEEDING - LIFE THREATENING
Severe bleeding can kill in minutes. This is the #1 priority after ensuring safety.

### Direct Pressure Method
1. Apply firm, constant pressure directly on the wound using the cleanest material available
2. Use your hand if nothing else is available — saving life is more important than infection risk
3. Press HARD — do not lift to check for at least 10-15 minutes
4. If blood soaks through, add more material ON TOP — never remove the first layer
5. Maintain pressure until bleeding stops or help arrives

### Improvised Bandage Materials (when no medical supplies)
- Clean cloth, t-shirt, headscarf (keffiyeh/shemagh)
- Sanitary pads or diapers (excellent absorbency)
- Plastic bag pressed over wound then cloth on top (creates seal)
- Paper towels layered thick
- Even dry leaves or moss in extreme wilderness situations

### Tourniquet — For Limb Bleeding That Won't Stop
A tourniquet is needed when direct pressure fails on arms or legs.
1. Place tourniquet 2-3 inches (5-7 cm) ABOVE the wound, never on a joint
2. Use a belt, strip of cloth (at least 2 inches wide), or rope
3. Tie it tight enough to stop the bleeding completely
4. Note the time it was applied — critical for medical teams later
5. Do NOT loosen once applied
6. A properly applied tourniquet WILL cause pain — this is normal and necessary
WARNING: Narrow materials like wire or string can cut into flesh — use the widest material available.

### Nose Bleeding
1. Sit upright and lean slightly forward (NOT backward — prevents swallowing blood)
2. Pinch the soft part of the nose firmly for 10-15 minutes without letting go
3. Apply cold compress to the bridge of the nose if available
4. Spit out any blood in the mouth

### Internal Bleeding Signs (cannot be treated without surgery)
- Abdomen becomes rigid, swollen, or painful after injury
- Bruising appears without external wound
- Vomiting blood or blood in stool
- Dizziness, confusion, rapid weak pulse
- Cold, clammy, pale skin
ACTION: Keep person lying down, legs elevated, keep warm, get to hospital urgently

## WOUND CARE

### Cleaning a Wound
1. Wash your hands first if possible
2. Rinse wound with clean water — use large amounts, gentle pressure
3. Remove visible debris (dirt, glass) with clean tweezers or fingers
4. Do NOT try to remove deeply embedded objects
5. If no clean water: use boiled then cooled water, or even urine in extreme emergencies (controversial but better than leaving a dirty wound)

### Wound Closure (without stitches)
- Butterfly strips: Cut thin strips of adhesive tape/plaster, pull wound edges together
- Super glue (cyanoacrylate): Can close small, clean cuts — apply thin layer to closed wound edges
- Hair: For scalp wounds, tie hair from each side together across the wound
- Do NOT close dirty/infected wounds — they need to drain

### Signs of Wound Infection (appears 1-3 days after injury)
- Increasing redness spreading from wound
- Swelling and warmth around wound
- Pus (yellow/green discharge)
- Red streaks moving away from wound toward heart
- Fever, chills
- Increasing pain rather than decreasing
TREATMENT: Keep wound clean, apply warm compresses, change dressings frequently. Honey (raw) has natural antibacterial properties and can be applied to infected wounds.

### Puncture Wounds (nails, wire, shrapnel)
1. Do NOT remove large embedded objects — stabilize them in place
2. Let small puncture wounds bleed briefly to flush bacteria
3. Clean around the wound thoroughly
4. Watch carefully for infection — puncture wounds are high risk
5. Tetanus risk is high — note for when medical care becomes available

## BURNS

### Burn Classification
- First degree: Red, painful, no blisters (like sunburn)
- Second degree: Blisters, very painful, red/white patches
- Third degree: White/brown/black, may be painless (nerve damage), waxy appearance

### Burn Treatment
DANGER: NEVER use warm water or hot water on a burn. NEVER heat water for a burn. Use COOL water ONLY.

Step 1: COOL the burn with COOL running water (NOT warm, NOT hot, NOT ice-cold) for 10-20 minutes. This is the most important step. COOL water reduces damage and pain.
Step 2: If no running water, soak clean cloth in COOL water and place on the burn. Keep rewetting the cloth to keep it COOL.
Step 3: Remove rings, watches, bracelets, clothing near the burn BEFORE swelling starts.
Step 4: Do NOT pop blisters — they protect the wound underneath.

DANGER — Things that must NEVER go on a burn:
- NEVER apply butter, oil, or grease (traps heat, causes more damage)
- NEVER apply toothpaste (causes infection)
- NEVER apply egg whites (infection risk)
- NEVER apply raw fruits or vegetables: NO cucumber, NO tomato, NO potato, NO onion (increases infection, does NOT help)
- NEVER apply flour, coffee grounds, or any food substance
- NEVER apply ice directly (causes frostbite on damaged skin)
All of the above are DANGEROUS MYTHS that make burns WORSE.

Step 5: Cover the burn with a clean, non-stick covering. Plastic wrap (cling film) works best. A clean dry cloth also works.
Step 6: For chemical burns, flush with large amounts of water for 20+ minutes.

SAFE things to put on a burn (ONLY these):
- COOL clean water (most important)
- Honey (raw) — has natural antibacterial properties and helps healing
- Aloe vera gel directly from the plant
- Clean plastic wrap or clean dry cloth as covering
Nothing else. If you only have water, that is enough.

### Burn Care Without Supplies
- Aloe vera plant gel (if available) — natural burn treatment
- Honey — antibacterial, promotes healing, reduces scarring
- Clean plastic bag/wrap — prevents infection, doesn't stick
- Elevate burned limbs to reduce swelling

### When Burns Are Emergencies
- Burns covering more than 10% of body (palm of hand = ~1%)
- Burns on face, hands, feet, joints, or genitals
- Burns that go all the way around a limb
- Any third-degree burn
- Chemical or electrical burns
- Burns with difficulty breathing (inhalation injury)
