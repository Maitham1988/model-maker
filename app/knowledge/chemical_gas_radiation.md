# Chemical, Gas & Radiation Exposure

## GAS EXPOSURE — GENERAL

### Immediate Actions
1. LEAVE THE AREA — move upwind and uphill (gases are usually heavier than air)
2. Cover nose and mouth with WET cloth — dampening improves filtration
3. Breathe through the cloth with slow, shallow breaths
4. If indoors: get outside immediately. If outdoors near a gas source: move away
5. Remove contaminated clothing as soon as safe to do so
6. If eyes are burning: flush with clean water for 15-20 minutes

### Improvised Gas Protection (NO Gas Mask Available)
- Wet cloth over nose and mouth (reduces some particles and water-soluble gases)
- Multiple layers of cloth = better filtration
- Breathe through a water-soaked shirt, keffiyeh, or towel
- In extreme emergency: breathe through a water-soaked piece of bread (traditional wartime tip)
- Stay LOW — most war gases are heavier than air, settle downward
- If indoors: seal doors/windows with wet cloth, plastic sheeting, tape
- Move to upper floors (gas sinks)
WARNING: Improvised protection is LIMITED. It reduces exposure but does NOT eliminate it.

### Signs You've Been Exposed to Gas
- Eyes: burning, tearing, blurred vision, pain
- Respiratory: coughing, choking, difficulty breathing, chest tightness
- Skin: redness, blistering, burning sensation
- Neurological: headache, dizziness, confusion, seizures, unconsciousness
- Gastrointestinal: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea

## SPECIFIC GAS TYPES

### Tear Gas (CS Gas) — Most Common
Used in crowd control. Not usually lethal but very painful.
1. Leave the area — move to fresh air
2. Face into the wind — helps clear residue from face
3. Do NOT rub eyes — spreads the irritant
4. Flush eyes with clean water or saline — pour from inner corner outward
5. Wash face and exposed skin with soap and water (cold water — hot opens pores)
6. Remove and bag all contaminated clothing
7. Symptoms typically resolve in 30-60 minutes in fresh air
8. Baby shampoo solution (diluted) can help break down CS particles on skin

### Chlorine Gas (Greenish-Yellow, Pool/Bleach Smell)
Heavier than air, settles in low areas.
1. Move to HIGH GROUND immediately — upper floors, hilltops
2. Cover nose/mouth with cloth soaked in BAKING SODA SOLUTION if available
3. If not available: wet cloth works as partial protection
4. Flush eyes with large amounts of water
5. Remove all contaminated clothing
6. Skin exposure: wash with soap and large amounts of water
7. If breathing is difficult: sit upright, breathe slowly, stay calm
8. Delayed pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs) can occur 2-24 hours after exposure — monitor breathing

### White Phosphorus (Burns on Contact with Air)
Used in some munitions. Causes severe chemical burns.
1. Particles continue burning until deprived of oxygen
2. Submerge affected area in water OR cover with wet mud/cloth
3. Do NOT try to pick out particles — they reignite when exposed to air
4. Keep wounds wet at all times
5. Remove particles only when UNDERWATER
6. Do NOT apply oil-based ointments — they can worsen absorption

### Carbon Monoxide (Odorless, Colorless)
From fires, generators, vehicle exhaust in enclosed spaces.
1. Move person to fresh air immediately
2. If unconscious: check breathing, start CPR if needed
3. Headache, dizziness, confusion, cherry-red skin = CO poisoning
4. Open all doors/windows
5. Turn off any combustion sources (generators, stoves, heaters)
6. Person needs medical oxygen — get to hospital if possible

### Ammonia (Sharp, Pungent Smell)
1. Move to fresh air
2. Flush eyes with water for 15-20 minutes
3. Flush skin with water for 15-20 minutes
4. Do NOT induce vomiting if swallowed
5. Keep person warm and calm

## CHEMICAL BURNS ON SKIN

### Treatment
1. Remove contaminated clothing while flushing with water
2. Flush with LARGE amounts of water for at least 20 minutes — more is better
3. Do NOT try to neutralize chemicals (acid with base or vice versa) — this causes more heat
4. Remove jewelry/watches near the area before swelling
5. After flushing: cover with clean, dry, loose dressing
6. For dry chemical powders: BRUSH OFF the powder FIRST, then flush with water
7. Eyes exposed to chemicals: flush with water for 30+ minutes, blinking frequently

## DECONTAMINATION (After Chemical/Gas Exposure)

### Self-Decontamination Steps
1. Move to a clean area upwind from the source
2. Remove ALL clothing — cut off over the head if needed (don't pull contaminated clothes over face)
3. Seal clothing in a plastic bag if possible
4. Wash entire body with large amounts of water and soap
5. Start from head, work downward — contaminated water flows away from face
6. Pay special attention to: hair, armpits, groin, between fingers/toes
7. If no running water: use wet wipes, damp cloth, or even dry flour/talcum powder to absorb liquid chemicals
8. Put on clean clothes
9. Wash yourself again if symptoms persist

## RADIATION EXPOSURE (Dirty Bomb, Nuclear)

### If You Suspect Radioactive Material
1. Get INSIDE a solid building — concrete/brick is best protection
2. Close all doors, windows, and ventilation
3. Stay in the CENTER of the building, away from windows and exterior walls
4. Upper floors of multi-story buildings are good for fallout (particles settle on ground)
5. BASEMENT is best overall — maximum shielding
6. Stay inside for at least 24 hours — radioactive fallout decays rapidly in the first hours

### Removing Radioactive Contamination
1. Remove and bag all outer clothing — this removes ~90% of contamination
2. Shower with soap and warm water — scrub gently, don't break skin
3. Wash hair with shampoo — do NOT use conditioner (binds particles to hair)
4. Blow nose, wipe eyelids and ears with damp cloth
5. Put on clean clothes from inside the building

### Potassium Iodide (KI) — If Available
- Protects thyroid gland from radioactive iodine
- ONLY effective if taken before or shortly after exposure
- Adults: 130mg dose, children: 65mg
- Does NOT protect against other types of radiation
- Only take if specifically instructed by authorities
